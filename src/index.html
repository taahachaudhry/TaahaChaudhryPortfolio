Ⅳ: Technology Resource Blog & Site And Conference

For this project, I build a Wordpress theme for a client who needed a place to display Data Science Technology resources, a blog, and projects. The client wanted a way to update blog articles and projects, so we needed to use a CMS and decided to use Wordpress. I began by first creating static HTML templates, styling the templates, and adding functionality, such as animate typing of the headers, with JavaScript. Afterwards, I used the PHP to 'Wordpressify' the site so that it would dynamically display blog posts from the database. Another senior developer implemented adding new blog posts and projects, through the Wordpress admin panel.

This client also held a Data Science and Engineering conference throughout different cities, so we developed an HTML template in this Wordpress theme to display conference marketing information and a few tables displaying the conference schedule and talks. Initally this HTML template was over 4000 lines of content, but I took the initiative to remove the data into PHP arrays in a separate file, and creating a PHP template to loop over the data to display the correct information for each table and talk. This reduced the HTML template to about 300 lines and it became much easier to accomodate the client's requests to add a talk or update one by just updating the data file.

WATCH

Apple watch prototype

Prototype:

Objective

At Idean, we were given a one week prototyping challenge, to design an Apple Watch app that would control a fully auto-piloted palm-sized selfie-drone, called the Wasp, created by a 'company' called WaspAir.

Design Process

The first task I did when beginning this prototype, and what I like to generally do before starting any project, is to understand the requirements, goal, and features. By doing this, I can ask any questions and clarifications I may have early on, which leads to a more effecient way of building a product. Essentially, the Wasp begins flying by throwing it in the air. It has four different actions, which are as follows: 1) the ability to change its flight mode between follow and orbit, 2) doing a 'fly by', where the Wasp zooms past the user, 3) 'return', where the Wasp comes in a reachable distance to pick up and automatically turn off, and 4) 'highlight', which highlights the last two minutes of recording for easy editing. The watch app should also display battery life and time, flight mode, and the drone location in relation to the user.

After understanding the requirements, I began reading the Apple Watch Human Interface Guidelines, as this is a new product, to understand the interface features and interactions available to me.

Solution

I began with hand sketches, and I realized I wanted this to be as simple as possible for the user to use and remember, especially considering that it is a new device on the market. I decided that the landing page would contain information pertaining to the drone, such as battery life, time remaining, and mode with speed. I also placed a navigation bar to always display the battery life and mode. By holding on the home screen, the other controls show up that a user can select. By swiping right, one can toggle between the flight modes, and by swiping left, it displays a compass showing the location relative to the user, along with the distance and altitude.

Through this challenge, I learned to read product design guidelines and the ability to using a prototyping tool to make the designs more interactive. I also learned to rapidly build an MVP, knowing that iterations and improvements can be made.

Portfolio Redesign

Web portfolio site application design

Code Repository: Site:

Objective

To redesign and develop my portolio site for a better, simpler, and more concise User Experience and User Interface for recruiters, developers, and users to be able to view my skills, work, and resume.

Design Process

I began this redesign by collecting feedback on my current portfolio site from other designers and recruiters. Two points were mainly derived from the feedback that I recieved. One is to remove unnecessary features and visual aesthetics (such as the hero section) to create a more concise and simpler UX. The second point is to understand what the content the users wants to see and restructuring the hierachy.

Based on the feedback that I gained from the user research that I conducted, I began writing out ideas and creating solutions to redesign the site. Thereafter, I began sketching low-to-mid fidelity wireframes and implementing the visuals using Sketch.

Solution

I first began by listing out the important components, which would dictate the structure of the application. The most important component is the display of my work, which is the purpose of this application, should be the first thing that users should be able to see, upon entering the site. Each project should also contain a process or walkthrough, or even a description, and anything else related to the projects. Other components included the about section, resume, and contact.

By understanding the components for the application and the feedback I recieved, I created ideas of how I was going to build the wireframes. I decided to remove unnecessary items, such as the hero components or quote, and remove animated functionality, and combine items where possible, such as displaying my skills through the project descriptions. I also wanted to create a smooter way to navigate through the site, so I decided to have a left-side navigation panel, which would also contain my contact information and social links. I also came up with a better hierarchy and structure for displaying project content.

Development

I decided to build this site myself using HTML, Sass, and JavaScript, along with some libraries, such as Animate.css, jQuery, and Masonry. I also used Gulp to build tasks, such as compiling my Sass files, linting, and browserify to use require in the browser by bundling up my dependencies. The biggest advantage I had using Gulp was a way to create a separate directory, which contained the HTML files, compiled Sass files, JavaScript files, and assets. By having this separate directory of compiled code, I was able to use Github pages to host my site, while still being able to code using Gulp and SCSS, and commit code as I normally would to a main repository.

Objective

[freespace] is a way to connect those with unused or underutilized space with the local community for civic innovation.
During the two day ATX Hack for a Change Hackathon (June 2014) we were challenged by [freespace]ATX to design and develop a platform for users to be able to either request or offer a space to use.

Process

We were a team of about 10 developers and designers. Considering this was a two day hackathon, we had to work together to get an minimum viable product out by the end of the weekend. The first thing we did is sit together and understand the non-profit and existing site. Thereafter, we figured out the most important components that can be built in the span of two days, which was the marketing page and the ability to request and offer a space. Two teams were formed at this point, the designers and the developers.

Solution

Since most of us developers had a Ruby on Rails background, we unanimously decided to build it on that framework and host in on Heroku. While a few developers began writing the UI structure the designers had mocked up, I, along with a few other back-end developers sat together and figured out the database schema and classes of the application. We created a task board containing a backlog, current, and completed tasks, and assigned tasks to ourselves. I took on the tasks to build aspects relating to the user from the user login and initial user dashboard, aling with a few admin features.
Tech Stack: Ruby on Rails, Twitter Bootstrap, Heroku, PostgreSQL Database
Note: [freespace]ATX may have reverted back to the general WP site, as no was maintaining the site after the hackathon.

Takeaways

I learned quite a few things at my first big hackathon. The first was being to build a MVP in two days by working with such a large team. This was also my first time collaborating with other designers and having to make updates and changes as they were given to us, and seeing the product evolve from mockups to an actual visually appealing product. I also realized the benefit we would have had of conducting better research and asking questions, such as maintenance of the site after the hackathon was over, to better build a product.
